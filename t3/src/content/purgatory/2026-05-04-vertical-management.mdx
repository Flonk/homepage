"Project managers" have always been odd to me. Like, the kind whose job it is to "manage" 5 different projects. If you have to keep so much in your head that you can't ever get into the meat of any single project, then what is your value exactly? Is it the impeccable jiraing? Second-to-none alignment meeting skills?

Doubtful.

The thing is, these people often appear to be both smart and super busy. So they must do important things, right? At the same time, from my lower-caste viewpoint it often seems like they just come in to make reports, then ask me about weekend plans or whatever, then they forward these reports to some higher-up.

I could have done that without the middle-man. Couldn't I have? Is there something that I'm not seeing?

Well, I have gotten to work with a few of these people and gathered enough data points now to see how these roles emerge. And it's never the case that some higher-up brought in a person to do jira grooming on 5 boards. Here is how it goes.

1. You do a few projects well and get new projects to do.
2. Since no project is ever done you end up with old *and* new projects.
3. You suddenly have 5 projects.
4. You can't go in depth on any of them so you try to own only the biggest decisions for each.
5. You stop having in-depth knowledge, and as a result are threatened to lose what made you perform well in the first place. You are at an impasse, and these are your options:
    - Become a micromanager, a.k.a the busy beaver
    - Become a parrot, a.k.a The transitive Jira bot
    - Refocus and/or change the org structure


## The transitive Jira bot

You are firmly in the management layer and intend to keep it that way -- the pay is good after all. Unfortunately you have no valuable input to give in your projects; you also don't have enough knowledge to make informed decisions and give advice to your superiors, so the best you can do is collect reports by people who know what they are doing, and forward them to higher-ups, so you can "make decisions together".

To make sure nobody realizes that the org could do without you, you have to become an information barrier between the people below and above you.

What do I mean by that?

Onto your inferiors, projects a strong "management" position. You are the guy that "takes the blow" if things go wrong; you are the person that protects the workers from the higher-ups, you are the tower of strength. Next, make sure your superiors always go through you and never talk to the project people so they don't realize that the project people know more about the project than you.

## The busy beaver

You are firmly in the management layer and intend to keep it that way -- the pay is good after all. Unfortunately, staying on top of what's happening in 5 projects is hard. You do your darndest: Work long hours, read through all the jira tickets; take time from project people to have them explain things to you, so you can make decisions. 

In the best case you echo your team's advice, so your decision exists just to justify your management. In the worst case, with incomplete information you make all the wrong decisions and destabilize your team, which would have been perfectly capable of making better decisions on their own.

Step 3: burn out.
